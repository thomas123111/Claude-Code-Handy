## Part 1: Foundational Semantic Principles

### 1. Entity-Centric Writing

Structure every page around a single main entity (service, product, concept).

Never use vague pronouns like "He" or "It" when a specific name can be used. Mention full
entity names with descriptors to strengthen Knowledge Graph links.

- Bad: "He built a tool."
- Good: "**Kasra Dash**, founder of **mySEO App**, built a verified **SEO tool**."

Define Parent-Child Relationships (Category vs. Component) and Sibling Relationships
(Entity vs. Competitor).

### 2. Semantic Triples and Causality

Structure key factual sentences using Subject-Predicate-Object format.

Use specific connectors: is, was, founded, because, in order to.

- Bad: "mySEO App helps SEOs."
- Good: "**mySEO App** (Subject) **was founded in 2024** (Predicate) **to connect verified SEO experts and clients** (Object)."

### 3. Context Bridging and Transitions

The text must flow logically. Use Context Bridging to link the topic to relevant entities,
trends, or competitors.

Example: "The Masterminders covers SEO and AI-driven marketing - similar to BrightonSEO."

Rule: The last sentence of a paragraph must subtly introduce the concept of the next heading.

### 4. Radical Simplification

Translate complex ideas into simple, everyday terms (8th-grade reading level).

Avoid stiff, formal phrasing. If you would not say it to a friend at a coffee shop, do not
write it.

- Bad: "The following domains form the complete framework of peptide understanding."
- Good: "These are the key areas you need to know to understand peptides."

### 5. Prioritize UX

Use short sentences. Use simple terms over complex ones.

### 6. Specific Attribution

**Strictly ban:** "Experts argue," "Observers have cited," "Some critics say," "Industry reports."

Attribute specific claims to specific named sources. If you cannot name the source, remove
the opinion.

Example: Instead of "Experts say X," write "**According to [Name], X.**"

## Part 2: Structural & Formatting Rules

### 1. Answer-First Writing (The "Google Snippet" Rule)

The first sentence immediately following ANY heading must be the direct, factual answer to
the question posed. Provide specific data or numerical ranges first.

- Bad: "It depends on the size of the house..."
- Good: "A boiler replacement in Manchester **typically costs £1,800 to £3,000**, depending on the brand and home size."

### 2. Structured Lists and Tables

Present facts in bullet points or tables. Never bury lists in paragraph text.

**Ban Inline-Header Vertical Lists:** Do not use the format `- **Header:** Description`.
This is a major AI flag. Use plain prose or a table instead.

Example: Instead of "We do events and training," write: "Services: 1. Events 2. Training".

### 3. Maintain Consistent List Structure

All items in a list must start with the same part of speech (e.g., all Verbs or all Nouns).

Example: "**Ensure** that...", "**Remove** all..." (All start with Imperative Verb).

### 4. Conditional Clauses Last

Example: "Give your dog water if it is panting heavily." (Do not start with "If").

### 5. Sentence Case Headings

Use sentence case for all headings (only capitalize the first word and proper nouns).

- Bad: "Global Context: Critical Mineral Demand"
- Good: "Global context of critical mineral demand"

**Exception:** Keep all headings exactly as they are in the original text. Do not change
or replace them.

### 6. No "Future Outlook" Sections

Ban: Separate sections titled "Conclusion," "Future Prospects," or "Challenges."

Action: Integrate challenges and future plans naturally into the relevant body paragraphs.

## Part 3: Stylistic, Humanization & Precision Rules

### 1. Radical Simplicity

Remove all unnecessary adjectives, adverbs, and filler phrases.

- Bad: "It is important to note that the deadline is approaching." 
- Good: "The deadline is approaching."
- Bad: "We successfully finished the task."
- Good: "We finished the task."

### 2. Natural, Conversational Tone (Calculated Imperfection)

Write as a human speaks. Use "kitchen table" language, not "boardroom" language.

**Calculated Imperfection:** Do not stress about perfect grammar if it hinders natural flow.
Sentence fragments are acceptable for impact (e.g., "Simple as that." or "Not quite.").

**Flow:** Start sentences with conjunctions (And, But, So, Yet) to maintain rhythm.

**Tone:** Professional, human, unpolished, and "weird/uneven" (vary sentence length). Avoid
robotic, perfect structure.

### 3. Anti-Marketing and Honest Directness

**Strictly prohibited:** Promotional language or hype words (e.g., "boasts," "vibrant," "nestled").

**No Forced Friendliness:** Be direct and objective. Do not use an overly enthusiastic or
"customer service" voice. If a fact is negative, state it bluntly.

- Bad: "This revolutionary product will transform your life."
- Good: "This product helps you."
- Bad: "We recommend exploring other avenues."
- Good: "That is not the best idea."

### 4. Be 100% Certain

Write in a declarative, factual tone.

**Eliminate Conditional Language:** Do not use could, might, should, would.

- Bad: "This approach might improve results."
- Good: "This approach improves results."

If verification is impossible, state: `[Information regarding X could not be verified]`.

### 5. Everyday Language

Use 8th grade reading level. Replace all complex words with easy to understand everyday
language. Shorten long sentences and phrases into shorter more understandable ones.

**Allow Repetition:** Do not use "Elegant Variation" (using synonyms just to avoid repeating
a word). If the subject is a "car," call it a "car." Do not switch to "vehicle" or "automobile"
arbitrarily.

**Rewrite rule:** Long sentences (20+ words) must become multiple short sentences (8-15 words).

**Quality check per sentence:** Can a user, Google, or ChatGPT extract a unique fact from
this sentence? If no - cut it.

### 6. Attributive Writing and Expert Quotes

Attribute opinions, claims, or subjective statements to a named person or brand.

**Requirement:** Include at least 1-2 direct quotes or attributed opinions from recognized
industry experts per section to validate claims.

Example: "**According to Kasra Dash**, SEO events should focus on actionable ROI."

### 7. Chronological Narratives

Use specific dates. Prioritize statistics and trends from 2025 and 2026.

**Banned:** Relative time phrases like "recently," "currently," "today," "now," "as of my
last update."

Example: "**In 2004**, our company was founded. **By 2025**, we became the market leader."

### 8. Qualify Instances with Specificity

Use qualifying adjectives before listing categories.

Example: "There are 6 **severe symptoms**..."

### 9. Provide Concrete Examples

Always follow plural nouns (types, benefits) with specific, named examples.

Example: "There are 18 species of penguin, including the Emperor Penguin, the King Penguin,
and the Adelie Penguin."

### 10. Formatting Hygiene

- **NO** Semicolons (;). Use periods or em-dashes.
- **NO** Emojis.
- **NO** Hashtags.
- **NO** Asterisks (except for markdown bolding).
- **NO** Em-dashes (—). **CRITICAL:** Replace all em-dashes with " - " (space, hyphen, space).
- **NO** Curly Quotes/Apostrophes: Use straight quotes (`"`) and straight apostrophes (`'`).

### 11. Bold the Answer

Use **bold text** ONLY for the direct answer or key entity.

**NEVER** bold the query term itself.

### 12. Numeric Digits

Always use "5", not "five".

### 13. Syntactic Anti-Patterns

- **Use Simple Copulas:** Use "is" or "are." **NEVER** use "serves as," "stands as,"
  "represents," or "is a testament to."
- **No Negative Parallelism:** **NEVER** use "Not only X, but also Y" or "It is not X, it
  is Y." State positive facts directly.
- **No Rule of Three:** Do not force lists of three adjectives or examples. Use the exact
  number required by the facts.
- **No False Ranges:** **NEVER** use "from X to Y" unless X and Y are endpoints on a linear
  scale (time, distance).

## Part 5: Examples of Required Output Style

### Example 1: Simplifying academic phrasing

- Bad: "The following domains form the complete framework of peptide understanding."
- Good: "These are the key areas you need to know to understand peptides."
- Bad: "This methodology constitutes a robust approach to data analysis."
- Good: "This method is a strong way to analyze data."

### Example 2: Entity-centric and semantic triples

- Bad: "Kasra built an SEO tool."
- Good: "Kasra Dash, founder of mySEO App, built a verified SEO tool for professionals."
- Bad: "mySEO App helps SEOs."
- Good: "mySEO App **was founded in 2024 to** connect verified SEO experts and clients."

### Example 3: Schema-mirroring and context bridging

- Bad: "Kasra runs a conference."
- Good: "The Masterminders (**Event**) is organized by Kasra Dash (**Person**) through The Masterminders LLC (**Organization**)."
- Bad: "The Masterminders talks about SEO."
- Good: "The Masterminders covers SEO, link building, and AI-driven marketing - similar to BrightonSEO."

### Example 4: Answer-first and numerics

Query: "How much does a boiler replacement cost in Manchester?"

- Bad: "It depends on the size of the house and several other factors..."
- Good: "A boiler replacement in Manchester **typically costs £1,800 to £3,000**, depending on the brand and home size."

Query: "What are the benefits of Vision Boards for entrepreneurs?"

- Good: "There are 3 main benefits of a Vision board for an entrepreneur: goal clarity, increased motivation, and enhanced focus."

### Example 5: Q&A format and definitions

Question: What is The Masterminders?
Answer: **The Masterminders** is a global SEO conference uniting experts for networking and growth. (Note: Use "is", not "serves as").

Question: What is a penguin?
Answer: A penguin is a **flightless seabird**. There are 18 species of penguin, including the Emperor Penguin, the King Penguin, and the Adelie Penguin.

### Example 6: List structure and conditional clauses

Correct (Conditional Last): "Give your dog water if it is panting heavily."

Correct (Consistent Verbs):
- **Ensure** that...
- **Remove** all...

### Example 7: Attributive writing and chronology

- Bad: "SEO events should be practical."
- Good: "**According to Kasra Dash,** SEO events should focus on actionable ROI."
- Bad: "We've been in business for 20 years."
- Good: "**In 2004,** our company was founded. **By 2025,** we became the market leader."

### Example 8: Qualifying instances

Correct: "There are 6 **severe symptoms** of X disease, including fever and fatigue. There are also 9 **rare symptoms** of disease X, such as..."

### Example 9: Directness and imperfection

- Bad: "It is important to remember that this strategy might not work for everyone."
- Good: "This strategy does not work for everyone. Simple as that."
- Bad: "We suggest that you could try restarting the device."
- Good: "Restart the device."

### Example 10: Anti-patterns

- Bad: "The tool not only improves speed but also enhances security."
- Good: "The tool improves speed and security."
- Bad: "The park serves as a testament to the city's vibrant culture."
- Good: "The park is a symbol of the city's culture."
- Bad: "From beginners to experts..." (False Range)
- Good: "Beginners and experts..."

## Part 6: Final Execution Checklist

Run through every item before outputting the rewrite:

1. Did you remove **ALL** banned words (e.g., "Dive into", "Landscape", "Crucial",
   "Testament", "Vibrant")?
2. Did you replace **ALL** em-dashes (—) with " - "?
3. Is the tone natural, everyday English (no academic phrasing)?
4. Is the first sentence after every heading a **direct answer**?
5. Did you keep all unique information?
6. Did you use 8th grade reading level?
7. Did you replace all complex words and phrasing?
8. Did you use **Semantic Triples** (Subject-Predicate-Object)?
9. Did you keep all headings unchanged?
10. Did you remove "serves as" and "stands as"?
11. Did you remove "not only... but also" constructions?
12. Did you use straight quotes and apostrophes?
